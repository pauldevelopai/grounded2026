AI carelessly used to draft South Africa’s AI policy

In a display of beautiful irony (or a purposeful teaching point) it was revealed over the weekend that South Africa’s Draft National AI Policy was generated (at least partly) with AI and as a result is full of fabricated references ( News24 found at least six). This proclivity to hallucinate links and sources is the most glaring shortcoming of using LLMs for research. The scandal unfolded much like any other “Used AI When They Shouldn’t Have” incident: The Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies, Solly Malatsi said he was “investigating the matter”. Khusela Diko, Chairperson of Parliament’s Portfolio Committee on Communications and Digital Technologies said that they should withdraw the policy (and they have since done so).

There is, of course, a wonderful farce in watching politicians try to explode blame away from themselves, talking about the power of AI technologies, while grappling with being caught because of the shortcomings of the tech, rather than any of its strengths. And you couldn’t have a firmer way to illustrate the shambolic fallout of being caught using AI than how the SA government has had to stutter apologies in the last few days. However, the fact that Minister Malatsi still has a job after this weekend is dumbfounding. Hearing him babble on about the need for human oversight in AI when his department released a policy suggesting all that, but refused to even uphold what they were suggesting during the making of the same policy… if anything marks the need for internal regulation and oversight without relying on government, this is it.

Malatsi also blamed AI in general as a reason for the fabricated references, but this incident puts the use of AI in clear terms: it will help you with your job, but won’t do everything for you. And no policy is really going to stop people who don’t care about their jobs or the work (like Malatsi and his department) from always taking the available short cut. Editors and media managers come to me with this concern often: they want their staff to sweat and care for the articles they produce and AI isn’t taking that away from them, but it is giving them an opportunity to expose their apathy.

There is a question of how many mistakes would Malatsi have made in this policy without AI? Would there be more than six incorrect references if it was left up to an entirely human staff? And if this was three years ago, in April 2023, when ChatGPT was new there would definitely have been many more entirely fabricated references. And in three years time there will probably be none. So government officials like Malatsi just need to hang on for the models to get a tad better… though then we won’t need the officials at all.

5 lessons on AI from The International Journalism Festival

I was on a panel two weeks ago with Will Church, Sannuta Raghu and Irene Jay Liu at The International Journalism Festival in Perugia, Italy. We talked through what AI can do (good and bad) for independent media. You can watch the full panel here . Huge thanks to Thomson Reuters Foundation for the trip and hosting the panel. We touched on how the data that newsrooms are sitting on has value, if they know how to manipulate it properly and make it suitable for an AI world. But these data nest eggs are not likely to provide the huge paydays from big tech companies that some newsrooms are still expecting. Sunita explained that for a small newsroom like Scroll.in , AI has been valuable because it allows limited teams to reach much larger audiences, especially through additional languages. But for her, success came from having a clear strategy, strong internal literacy and using open-source tools where possible, not blindly adopting expensive AI products. And I hammered home the point that many independent newsrooms, particularly in countries such as Zimbabwe and Zambia, wrongly think AI is about creating headlines and images, when the bigger opportunity lies in using newsroom data, archives and expertise to build services audiences may actually pay for.

The next AI panel I want to highlight was by colleagues from DW Akademie on the AI hype cycle . They have recently released a toolkit that helps journalists burrow past the hype and report efficiently on emerging technologies and AI with nuance. Jascha Bareis argued that hype is not simply excitement or optimism, it is the deliberate circulation of oversized claims about what technology will do. Because those claims concern the future, they are difficult to disprove in the present. This allows companies and influential figures to make dramatic promises about jobs, growth or transformation without any accountability. Karen Allen added that when it comes to countries such as South Africa and Kenya the AI reporting can swing from AI utopia to AI doom, while missing everyday realities. She said that AI coverage is driven by vendors promising solutions in health, education and development. But these stories frequently ignore poor infrastructure, weak regulation (see the story above), unemployment, language barriers and the fact that many systems are not designed for African contexts.

The next panel was with Florent Daudens and Lucky Gunasekara on how news revenue systems built around clicks, ad impressions and subscriptions are being dissolved by AI assistants and agents. Instead of users visiting websites, people increasingly ask chatbots questions and receive synthetic answers without clicking through. Existing tools such as robots.txt (which is meant to stop AI scraping) are now outdated, easy to ignore and poorly enforced. There was a lot of talk (as always) about AI eventually changing the formats of journalism or if news will become just data to be used how the LLMs feel fit (this was also touched on during the panel aptly titled How To Edit A Liquid ). However, there is a potential happy ending: open standards and protocols could help journalism rebuild leverage. Model Context Protocol (MCP) and payment layers mean that publishers could directly control what AI systems access, under what terms and potentially charge for it. Rather than relying on closed platform deals, open protocols could create a more transparent and scalable market for quality journalism in the AI economy.
